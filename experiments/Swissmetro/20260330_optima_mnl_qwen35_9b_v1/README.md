# Optima Multinomial Logit Experiment Report for qwen3.5:9b

This archive folder records a multinomial logit (MNL) estimation exercise based on the retained Optima data package and the artificial-intelligence (AI) questionnaire results generated by `qwen3.5:9b`. In the current repository organization, the maintained code lives in the root `scripts/` directory and the maintained data package lives in `data/Swissmetro/demographic_choice_psychometric`. The present folder should therefore be interpreted as an archival trial that stores the resulting estimation outputs rather than as the active development location.

The experiment estimated two MNL specifications on both the human benchmark data and the retained AI questionnaire data. The first specification is the basic four-parameter model, containing two alternative-specific constants and two generic marginal utility coefficients for cost and time. The second specification augments this baseline with respondent characteristics, while excluding destination and origin variables. In both cases, the available alternatives are `PT`, `CAR`, and `SLOW_MODES`.

## Archive structure

- `outputs/human_basic` stores the human benchmark estimation for the four-parameter MNL.
- `outputs/human_user_no_od` stores the human benchmark estimation for the respondent-characteristic MNL without destination and origin variables.
- `outputs/ai_basic` stores the AI-questionnaire estimation for the four-parameter MNL.
- `outputs/ai_user_no_od` stores the AI-questionnaire estimation for the respondent-characteristic MNL without destination and origin variables.

## Main numerical results

The human benchmark exhibits a substantial improvement when respondent characteristics are introduced. The final log likelihood improves from `-500.457` in the four-parameter model to `-391.212` in the respondent-characteristic model. This pattern indicates that the selected non-destination, non-origin covariates carry meaningful explanatory power in the human Optima sample.

The AI data behave very differently. In the four-parameter model, the final log likelihood is already `-17.960`, far above the human benchmark in absolute fit. Once respondent characteristics are introduced, the final log likelihood moves to `-0.000629`, which is essentially a near-perfect in-sample fit. This result should not be interpreted as evidence that the AI data are behaviorally more realistic. Instead, it indicates that the retained AI questionnaire responses are highly concentrated and therefore unusually easy to classify.

## Behavioral interpretation

The basic four-parameter model already reveals an important divergence between the human and AI data. In the human benchmark, both cost and time have negative coefficients. The estimated cost coefficient is approximately `-0.613`, and the estimated time coefficient is approximately `-0.339`. This pattern is consistent with a conventional utility-maximizing interpretation in which higher generalized cost reduces the probability of choosing an alternative.

By contrast, in the AI basic model the estimated cost coefficient is approximately `-0.073`, whereas the time coefficient becomes positive at approximately `0.160`. The positive time coefficient is behaviorally counterintuitive, and the cost coefficient is much closer to zero than in the human benchmark. This already suggests that the AI responses are not reproducing the human trade-off structure between time and cost.

The choice distributions reinforce this conclusion. In the retained human data, the shares are approximately `31.4%` for `PT`, `62.6%` for `CAR`, and `6.1%` for `SLOW_MODES`. In the retained AI data, the corresponding shares are approximately `4.9%` for `PT`, `94.6%` for `CAR`, and `0.4%` for `SLOW_MODES`. Therefore, the AI sample is overwhelmingly concentrated on `CAR`, leaving very little effective variation for the other two alternatives.

## Interpretation of the respondent-characteristic model

The respondent-characteristic specification should be read with caution for the AI sample. In the human benchmark, adding the fifteen retained covariates yields a plausible improvement in fit and generates moderate coefficient magnitudes. In the AI sample, however, the same specification produces extremely large coefficients and a near-zero final log likelihood. For example, several `B_CAR_*` coefficients move to very large positive or negative values, and even the generic cost coefficient becomes positive in the AI estimation. These patterns are more consistent with quasi-separation or overfitting than with stable behavioral structure.

Accordingly, the respondent-characteristic results are informative primarily as a diagnostic. They show that the AI questionnaire data are not merely different from the human benchmark in aggregate shares. Rather, the AI data appear to encode a much more deterministic and much more car-dominant decision rule, which allows the MNL model to classify almost all observations with minimal residual uncertainty.

## Overall assessment

This archived trial supports three concise conclusions. First, the Optima human benchmark behaves as expected under both the basic and the respondent-characteristic MNL specifications. Second, the retained `qwen3.5:9b` questionnaire data do not reproduce the human trade-off structure, because the AI sample is overwhelmingly concentrated on `CAR` and even generates an implausible positive time coefficient in the basic specification. Third, the near-perfect fit of the respondent-characteristic AI model should be interpreted as a warning sign of excessive determinism in the AI-generated responses rather than as a sign of successful behavioral replication.
